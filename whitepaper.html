Example: Let's say that a company issues an ERC20 token with a total supply of 10,000, and the current market price of the token is $1. The company decides to implement a buyback program, in which it will repurchase 1,000 tokens from the market at a price of $1.50 per token.

The total cost of the buyback program for the company would be 1,000 tokens * $1.50/token = $1,500. After the buyback, the total supply of the token will have been reduced to 10,000 tokens - 1,000 tokens = 9,000 tokens. ​

Assuming that the demand for the token remains the same, the reduced supply will lead to an increase in the price of the token. Let's say that the price of the token increases to $1.50 as a result of the buyback. The value of a holder's investment in the token will now be $1.50/token * 1,000 tokens = $1,500, which is the same as the cost of the buyback program for the company.

However, if the price of the token increases beyond $1.50, the holder's investment will be worth more than the cost of the buyback program. For example, if the price increases to $2/token, the value of the holder's investment will be $2/token * 1,000 tokens = $2,000, which is a $500 profit.
